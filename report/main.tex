\noindent
The two parts of this coursework address complementary questions about the
same class of phase-contrast microscopy data, a sequence of Glioblastoma
astrocytoma U373 cells acquired at fifteen-minute intervals with a pixel size
of \(0.65\,\mu\)m. Part~I treats the recovery of a clean image from a
diffraction-limited and noisy measurement, first with a classical variational
estimator and then with a learned unrolled solver. Part~II estimates where
motion occurs inside the field of view, localises the cells with a trained
segmentation network, and links the resulting detections into trajectories.
Throughout, I separate the mathematical model, the discretisation, and the
empirical behaviour, and I report the quantitative evidence that supports each
claim. All numbers quoted below are read directly from the metadata written by
the pipeline, so the report and the code remain consistent.

\section{Part I: Inverse Problems in Imaging}

\subsection{A.1 Forward problem: simulating image acquisition}

The acquisition of a diffraction-limited instrument is modelled as a linear
forward operator followed by additive noise,
\begin{equation}
  f = Hg + w, \qquad w \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma_n^2 I),
\end{equation}
where \(g\in\mathbb{R}^{m\times n}\) is the unknown clean image, \(H\) is a
convolution with the point-spread function (PSF), and \(f\) is the recorded
frame. The optical blur is modelled by a Gaussian PSF of standard deviation
\(\sigma=2.0\). A clean TIFF frame is loaded, cast to floating point, and
rescaled to \([0,1]\) by an affine min--max map so that the noise level and the
regularisation parameters have a consistent meaning across frames. The blur is
applied with \texttt{skimage.filters.gaussian}, additive zero-mean Gaussian
noise with \(\sigma_n=0.001\) is drawn from a generator seeded at \(42\), and
the result is clipped to the valid intensity range. Seeding makes the simulated
measurement reproducible, which matters when the same acquisition feeds the
restoration experiments.

Representing the blur by its modulation transfer function (MTF), the radially
averaged magnitude of the Fourier transform of the PSF, clarifies what the
inverse problem must undo. The MTF of the \(\sigma=2.0\) kernel falls to half
of its peak at roughly \(0.090\) cycles per pixel, equivalent to a period of
about \(11\) pixels or \(7.2\,\mu\)m at this pixel size
(Figure~\ref{fig:psf}). Structures finer than this scale are strongly
attenuated, so their recovery depends almost entirely on the prior rather than
on the data. The same observation explains why naive inversion is unstable, as
the normal operator \(H^\top H\) has very small singular values at high spatial
frequency, and dividing by them amplifies noise. This motivates the
regularised estimators that follow.

\subsection{A.2 Classical image restoration}

The classical estimator solves the Rudin--Osher--Fatemi total variation
denoising problem stated in the brief,
\begin{equation}
  \min_{g}\; \tfrac12\norm{g-f}_2^2 + \lambda\,\mathrm{TV}(g),
  \qquad
  \mathrm{TV}(g)=\sum_{x,y}\sqrt{(D_xg)^2+(D_yg)^2},
  \label{eq:rof}
\end{equation}
with \(D_x,D_y\) the forward horizontal and vertical difference operators under
Neumann boundary conditions. The discrete divergence is implemented as the
exact negative adjoint of this gradient, so that the primal and dual updates are
consistent. I solve \eqref{eq:rof} with the Chambolle--Pock first-order
primal--dual method~\cite{chambollepock}, which handles the non-smooth isotropic
TV term exactly through the proximal operator of its convex conjugate. That
proximal operator is the Euclidean projection of the dual field onto the
pointwise \(\ell_2\) ball of radius \(\lambda\), which is the step that couples
the regularisation strength into the iteration. The scheme is summarised in
Algorithm~\ref{alg:cp}. With the gradient operator norm bounded by
\(\norm{\nabla}\le\sqrt{8}\), the step sizes \(\tau=\sigma=0.25\) satisfy the
convergence condition \(\tau\sigma\norm{\nabla}^2<1\).

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Chambolle--Pock TV denoising for problem~\eqref{eq:rof}}
\label{alg:cp}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE observation \(f\), parameter \(\lambda\), steps \(\tau,\sigma\), relaxation \(\theta=1\)
\STATE \(x \leftarrow f\), \quad \(\bar x \leftarrow f\), \quad dual field \(p \leftarrow 0\)
\FOR{\(k=1\) to \(N\)}
  \STATE \(p \leftarrow p + \sigma\,\nabla \bar x\) \hfill (dual ascent)
  \STATE \(p \leftarrow p / \max\!\big(1,\, |p|/\lambda\big)\) \hfill (projection onto the \(\ell_2\) ball of radius \(\lambda\))
  \STATE \(x^{+} \leftarrow \big(x + \tau\,\mathrm{div}\,p + \tau f\big)/(1+\tau)\), \quad clip to \([0,1]\)
  \STATE \(\bar x \leftarrow x^{+} + \theta\,(x^{+}-x)\), \quad \(x \leftarrow x^{+}\)
\ENDFOR
\STATE \textbf{return} \(x\)
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{(i) Effect of the regularisation parameter.}
Three values of \(\lambda\) probe the under-regularised, balanced, and
over-regularised regimes. Small \(\lambda\) keeps the estimate close to the
measurement, so fine texture survives together with the residual noise, while
large \(\lambda\) drives the gradient field toward zero and flattens genuine
structure. The quantities in Table~\ref{tab:a2} show that on this frame the
weakest setting is numerically best and the strongest clearly over-smooths.
The reason is specific to this acquisition: the additive noise is very small
(\(\sigma_n=0.001\)), so the dominant error in \(f\) is the blur, and every
smoothing operation, including TV, can only remove information that the data
term is trying to keep. The error maps in Figure~\ref{fig:a2err} make this
concrete, as the reconstruction error is concentrated on cell boundaries for
all methods and changes only marginally between them. Critically, the
objective~\eqref{eq:rof} contains no forward operator \(H\); it is a denoising
model and cannot invert the Gaussian blur. The role of A.2 is therefore to
illustrate the noise-versus-detail trade-off rather than to deconvolve, which is
exactly the gap that the learned operator-aware model in Part~I.B closes.

\paragraph{(ii) Why TV preserves edges better than a Gaussian filter.}
A Gaussian filter is a linear low-pass operator that attenuates all high spatial
frequencies. Edges are broadband features, so they are smeared in proportion to
the kernel width regardless of their contrast. The TV penalty instead measures
the \(\ell_1\) norm of the gradient magnitude. Because this norm grows only
linearly with the height of a jump, a single sharp edge and a gradual ramp of
the same total amplitude incur the same penalty, and the data fidelity term is
free to retain the edge wherever the measurement supports it. The penalty does
suppress the small-amplitude oscillations that constitute noise, so TV removes
noise while keeping discontinuities, producing the characteristic piecewise
smooth reconstructions. In the present low-noise regime this advantage is muted,
which Table~\ref{tab:a2} reflects honestly.

\paragraph{(iii) Convergence of the objective.}
For each \(\lambda\) the objective value is recorded after every iteration
(Figure~\ref{fig:a2}(b)). The traces decrease monotonically and flatten,
which is the expected behaviour of a convergent primal--dual scheme on a convex
problem. The decrease is largest in the first few iterations and the residual
clipping to \([0,1]\) does not destabilise it. The larger \(\lambda\) starts
from a much higher objective because the TV term is weighted more heavily, yet
its relative reduction over the run is comparable, confirming that the solver is
approaching the minimiser of \eqref{eq:rof} rather than stalling.

\subsection{B. Data-driven regularisation by algorithm unrolling}

The data-driven model replaces the hand-chosen TV prior with a learned proximal
map and accounts explicitly for the blur. Proximal gradient descent on the
operator-aware objective \(\tfrac12\norm{Hg-f}_2^2 + R_\gamma(g)\) alternates a
gradient step on the data term with the proximal operator of the regulariser.
Unrolling truncates this iteration to \(K\) steps and replaces each proximal
operator by a small convolutional network, turning the solver into a trainable
feed-forward architecture,
\begin{equation}
  g^{(k+1)} = \varphi_{\theta^{(k)}}\!\Big(g^{(k)} - \eta^{(k)} H^\top\big(Hg^{(k)}-f\big)\Big),
  \qquad 0\le k\le K-1.
  \label{eq:unroll}
\end{equation}
I use \(K=6\). The blur \(H\) and its adjoint are implemented as a depthwise
convolution with a normalised Gaussian kernel; because the kernel is symmetric,
\(H^\top\) is the same padded convolution, which makes the data-term gradient
exact. Each \(\varphi_{\theta^{(k)}}\) is a three-layer residual CNN with two
PReLU activations and \(32\) feature channels, and the step sizes are kept
non-negative by the parameterisation \(\eta^{(k)}=\mathrm{softplus}(\beta^{(k)})\)
with learnable \(\beta^{(k)}\). The architecture is shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:arch} and the forward and training passes in
Algorithm~\ref{alg:unroll}. The network is trained by minimising the
\(\ell_1\) loss between the final iterate \(g^{(K)}\) and the clean reference,
using \(128\times128\) random crops with random horizontal and vertical flips
drawn from sequence~01, which compensates for the small number of training
images, while sequence~02 supplies the test metrics. Observations are generated
on the fly as \(f=Hg+w\) with noise variance \(0.001\).

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Unrolled PGD forward pass and training step}
\label{alg:unroll}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE observation \(f\), operator \(H\), CNNs \(\{\varphi_{\theta^{(k)}}\}\), raw steps \(\{\beta^{(k)}\}\)
\STATE \(g \leftarrow H^\top f\)
\FOR{\(k=0\) to \(K-1\)}
  \STATE \(\eta \leftarrow \mathrm{softplus}(\beta^{(k)})\) \hfill (ensures \(\eta\ge 0\))
  \STATE \(g \leftarrow \varphi_{\theta^{(k)}}\!\big(g - \eta\,H^\top(Hg-f)\big)\), \quad clip to \([0,1]\)
\ENDFOR
\STATE compute \(\mathcal{L}=\norm{g-g_{\text{true}}}_1\); backpropagate; Adam update of \(\{\theta^{(k)},\beta^{(k)}\}\)
\STATE \textbf{return} \(g\)
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{(i) Matched training and testing.}
With matched \(11\times11\), \(\sigma=2.0\) blur, the model was trained for
\(50\) epochs at batch size \(4\) and learning rate \(10^{-4}\) on a GPU. Across
all \(115\) test images the degraded input scores PSNR \(28.18\)\,dB and SSIM
\(0.490\), and the reconstruction raises this to PSNR \(34.10\)\,dB and SSIM
\(0.911\) (Figure~\ref{fig:bunroll}(a)). The six learned step sizes converge to nearly
equal values near \(0.13\), indicating that the network settles on an almost
constant descent step for the well-conditioned Gaussian normal operator and
delegates the remaining work to the learned proximal maps.

\paragraph{(ii) Out-of-distribution generalisation.}
Retraining the same independent-step architecture on a more severe degradation
(\(21\times21\), \(\sigma=4.0\)) and testing on the B(i) degradation isolates
sensitivity to a forward-model mismatch. The model reaches PSNR \(33.11\)\,dB
and SSIM \(0.903\), a drop of \(0.99\)\,dB and \(0.008\) SSIM relative to the
matched model (Figure~\ref{fig:bunroll}(b)). The learned solver therefore does not
fully transfer across operators despite the harder training blur. The learned
data term is tied to the training PSF through the \(H^\top H\) gradient, so a
test image whose blur is weaker is partially over-corrected. The degradation is
modest rather than catastrophic because the proximal CNNs encode a generic image
prior that remains useful, which is the practically relevant conclusion: the
prior generalises, the geometry of the data term does not.

\paragraph{(iii) Shared parameters and convergence beyond the training depth.}
Tying the proximal CNN and the step size across steps yields the iteration
\(g^{(k+1)}=\psi_\theta(g^{(k)}-\eta H^\top(Hg^{(k)}-f))\), trained at \(K=6\)
and then run for \(T=K,4K,8K,16K\) on one test image (Table~\ref{tab:b3},
Figure~\ref{fig:b3}). PSNR collapses from \(33.61\)\,dB at \(T=6\) to
\(6.56\)\,dB at \(T=96\). The learned map is optimised to reach a good image in
exactly six steps, not to be a contraction with the clean image as its fixed
point. Past the trained depth, the proximal CNN keeps removing what it treats as
residual structure, eroding genuine detail, and the iterates drift away. The
result shows that a finite-depth unrolled network should be read as a learned
\(K\)-step estimator rather than as a convergent optimisation algorithm, which is
the central caveat of the unrolling approach.

\section{Part II: Motion Estimation and Cell Tracking}

\subsection{A. Motion estimation}

\paragraph{(i) Image pair and change visualisation.}
I take the consecutive sequence-01 frames \texttt{t000} and \texttt{t001},
acquired fifteen minutes apart, and normalise both to \([0,1]\). A simple and
informative change map is the absolute difference \(|f_{t+1}-f_t|\), rescaled for
display (Figure~\ref{fig:motion}, top right). It lights up cell boundaries and
the textured interiors where intensity has shifted, while the static substrate
stays dark, giving a first qualitative picture of where motion is concentrated
before any flow is estimated.

\paragraph{(ii--iii) Horn--Schunck implementation and testing.}
The Horn--Schunck estimator~\cite{hornschunck} minimises the optical-flow
constraint together with a Tikhonov smoothness term,
\begin{equation}
  E(u,v)=\big\langle (D_xf\,u + D_yf\,v + D_tf)^2\big\rangle
        + \alpha^2\big\langle |\nabla u|^2+|\nabla v|^2\big\rangle .
  \label{eq:hs}
\end{equation}
Spatial derivatives \(D_xf,D_yf\) are taken on the average of the two frames and
the temporal derivative is \(D_tf=f_{t+1}-f_t\). The Euler--Lagrange equations of
\eqref{eq:hs} give the Jacobi update of Algorithm~\ref{alg:hs}, in which the
local average \(\bar u\) is the four-neighbour mean. That mean is exactly the
kernel \([[0,0.25,0],[0.25,0,0.25],[0,0.25,0]]\) suggested in the brief; I
implement it with edge padding so that border pixels are handled consistently.
The denominator \(\alpha^2+D_xf^2+D_yf^2\) is precomputed once. I verify the
estimator on a synthetic square translated by one pixel along each axis: the
recovered flow has the correct sign in the direction of motion and a
near-zero orthogonal component, with the magnitude attenuated by the smoothness
term and by the aperture problem, which is the expected qualitative behaviour
and the basis for the unit test that guards the implementation.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{Horn--Schunck optical flow (Jacobi iteration)}
\label{alg:hs}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE frames \(f_t,f_{t+1}\), regularisation \(\alpha\), iterations \(N\)
\STATE \(D_xf,D_yf \leftarrow \nabla\big(\tfrac12(f_t+f_{t+1})\big)\); \quad \(D_tf \leftarrow f_{t+1}-f_t\)
\STATE \(u,v \leftarrow 0\); \quad \(d \leftarrow \alpha^2 + D_xf^2 + D_yf^2\)
\FOR{\(k=1\) to \(N\)}
  \STATE \(\bar u,\bar v \leftarrow\) four-neighbour average of \(u,v\)
  \STATE \(r \leftarrow (D_xf\,\bar u + D_yf\,\bar v + D_tf)/d\)
  \STATE \(u \leftarrow \bar u - D_xf\cdot r\); \quad \(v \leftarrow \bar v - D_yf\cdot r\)
  \STATE record \(E(u,v)\) from \eqref{eq:hs}
\ENDFOR
\STATE \textbf{return} \(u,v,\;\text{energy trace}\)
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{(iv) Energy.}
For \(\alpha=1\) and \(100\) iterations the energy decreases from
\(1.859\times10^{-3}\) to a minimum of \(1.840\times10^{-3}\) at iteration
\(49\), then rises slightly to \(1.842\times10^{-3}\). The Jacobi sequence is
therefore not monotonically energy-decreasing once it overshoots, so the
minimum-energy iterate is a more principled stopping point than the final one.
The dashed marker in Figure~\ref{fig:motion} indicates this iterate.

\paragraph{(v) Estimated flow.}
The flow is shown both as an HSV map, in which hue encodes direction and
brightness encodes magnitude, and as a quiver overlay with vectors enlarged a
hundredfold for visibility. Motion concentrates on phase-contrast edges and
intracellular texture, while the background is nearly stationary. The
background-inclusive mean vector is \((\bar u,\bar v)=(-0.0026,-0.0043)\) pixels
per frame with mean magnitude \(0.020\) pixels, consistent with slow migration
sampled coarsely in time.

\paragraph{(vi) Effect of \(\alpha\) and the iteration count.}
The sweep over \(\alpha\in\{0.3,1,3\}\) and \(\{10,50,100,200\}\) iterations in
Figure~\ref{fig:motion} (bottom) separates the two controls. At \(100\)
iterations the mean magnitude falls from \(0.110\) pixels at \(\alpha=0.3\) to
\(0.020\) at \(\alpha=1\) and \(0.0026\) at \(\alpha=3\): stronger regularisation
both suppresses and spatially smooths the field. Iterations act as a diffusion
that propagates flow from textured regions into neighbouring pixels, but all
settings eventually pass their measured energy minimum, so more iterations are
not uniformly better. Together these observations motivate validating \(\alpha\)
against a known displacement and using energy-based early stopping.

\subsection{B. Cell detection and segmentation with YOLO}

\paragraph{(i) Conversion to YOLO segmentation format.}
The \texttt{01\_GT/SEG} and \texttt{02\_GT/SEG} label images are converted to
the normalised-polygon format that Ultralytics expects, using sequence~01 for
training (\(15\) frames) and sequence~02 for validation (\(19\) frames). Each
connected component is passed through \texttt{regionprops}, its longest contour
is extracted with \texttt{find\_contours} and simplified with
\texttt{approximate\_polygon}, and the vertices are normalised by the image
width and height. Padding the component before contouring avoids open contours at
the mask border. To verify the export I rasterise the written polygons back to
binary masks and compare them with the originals, obtaining a mean intersection
over union of \(0.9967\) and a minimum of \(0.9769\), so the conversion is
faithful (Figure~\ref{fig:yolo}(a)). The annotations cover only whole cell
bodies, so the model must learn to discard phase-contrast halos around the
cells, bright intracellular texture, small debris on the substrate, and the
partial cells clipped at the image border.

\paragraph{(ii) Training and validation.}
I fine-tune the pretrained \texttt{yolo11n-seg} checkpoint, the nano
segmentation model from Ultralytics~\cite{ultralytics}, for \(100\) epochs at
\(640\times640\) and batch size \(16\) with GPU acceleration; the brief's
\texttt{yolo26n-seg} is an example identifier and \texttt{yolo11n-seg} is the
corresponding available segmentation weight. On the \(19\) validation frames the
mask metrics are precision \(0.916\), recall \(0.895\), mAP\(_{50}\)(M)
\(0.902\), and the requested mAP\(_{50\text{--}95}\)(M) \(0.694\). The
precision--recall curve in Figure~\ref{fig:yolo}(b) is high and flat until recall
approaches \(0.9\), where precision falls sharply, which is the signature of a
detector that is accurate on the cells it finds but misses a fraction of them.

\paragraph{(iii) From predictions to a label image.}
Inference returns one polygon per detected instance. The conversion function
fills each polygon with \texttt{skimage.draw.polygon}, assigns it a distinct
positive integer, and leaves the background at zero, producing an integer label
image compatible with the downstream \texttt{regionprops} analysis. The
confidence threshold is \(0.25\).

\paragraph{(iv--v) Frame choice and quality.}
For a fair qualitative test I overlay predictions on the unannotated frames
\texttt{t000}, \texttt{t057}, and \texttt{t114}, sampling the start, middle, and
end of sequence~01 rather than replotting frames the model was trained on. These
frames return only two, three, and two masks despite further visible cell-like
objects (Figure~\ref{fig:yolopred}), and the validation prediction batch in
Figure~\ref{fig:yoloval} shows the same pattern of confident but incomplete
detection. The normalised confusion matrix in Figure~\ref{fig:yolo}(c)
quantifies this: at the inference threshold of \(0.25\) only \(0.18\) of the true
cells are recovered while \(0.82\) are assigned to background. The favourable
validation mAP and recall, which credit low-confidence detections by sweeping the
confidence threshold, therefore overstate the recall achievable at a fixed
operating point. Boundaries of the detected large cells are plausible, but recall
is poor for smaller, brighter, and partial cells, a limitation traceable to the
sparse annotations on only \(19\) frames.

\subsection{C. Cell tracking}

\paragraph{(i) Sequence segmentation.}
The trained model is loaded once and applied at confidence \(0.25\) to all
\(115\) frames of sequence~01, producing \(287\) masks in total with exactly one
frame returning no mask.

\paragraph{(ii) Centroids and the missing information.}
For every mask, \texttt{regionprops} supplies the centroid and area. What is
absent is temporal identity: nothing in a single frame says which detection at
time \(t+1\) is the same cell as a detection at time \(t\). Supplying that
correspondence, while coping with missed, merged, and split masks, is exactly
the job of the tracker.

\paragraph{(iii) LapTrack parameters and gap closing.}
Tracking uses \texttt{LapTrack}~\cite{laptrack} with squared-Euclidean linking
and gap-closing costs, a \(35\)-pixel cutoff (\(35^2=1225\) in squared distance),
a one-frame gap, and splitting and merging disabled because the cells neither
divide nor fuse on this timescale. At \(0.65\,\mu\)m per pixel the cutoff permits
\(22.75\,\mu\)m of displacement per fifteen-minute interval, which exceeds the
observed \(95\)th-percentile and maximum linked speeds of \(12.0\) and
\(24.2\) pixels per frame and so leaves margin for the fastest cells without
being so permissive that spurious links dominate. Gap closing is how the tracker
compensates for missing masks: when a cell is undetected in a single frame, the
algorithm links the detection before the gap to the one after it, bridging up to
\texttt{max\_gap} frames; longer omissions correctly terminate the track.

\paragraph{(iv) Visualisation and limitations.}
LapTrack returns \(43\) tracks with median length three frames, a maximum of
\(29\), and \(23\) tracks lasting at most three frames
(Figure~\ref{fig:track}). The composite shows the full trajectories on
\texttt{t000} with start and end markers, the number of detections per frame,
and the track-length distribution, the last of which exposes the severe
fragmentation directly. This fragmentation is the downstream consequence of the
YOLO under-detection documented above rather than a tracking failure: with two
or three detections per frame, many tracks cannot persist.

\paragraph{(v) Relating tracks to optical flow.}
The two analyses can be combined per cell and per frame interval by averaging
the dense Horn--Schunck flow inside each segmented mask and comparing that
internal-motion vector with the centroid displacement of the track. The angle
between them measures whether intracellular movement aligns with the direction of
migration, and the two magnitudes separate internal reorganisation from bulk
translation. Masks that are uncertain or filled by gap closing should be excluded
or down-weighted so that the comparison reflects measured motion only.

\section{Software engineering}

The code is an installable package under \texttt{src/image\_analysis\_coursework}
with thin command-line entry points declared in \texttt{pyproject.toml}, which
serves as the build manifest for a standard \texttt{setuptools} build. Each part
of the brief maps to one module and one script, the heavy GPU runs are submitted
through a Slurm wrapper, and all figures and metrics are written as files so the
report can quote them verbatim. Reproducibility is handled by seeding NumPy,
PyTorch, and Python for every experiment and by pinning the dataset download
through a known SHA-256 hash. Correctness is guarded by thirty unit tests
covering the gradient--divergence adjoint pair, the optical-flow derivatives and
energy decrease, the unrolled forward pass and step positivity, the YOLO export
round-trip, and the tracker's gap-closing logic; the suite runs on GitLab CI for
every push. Style is enforced with \texttt{ruff} through a \texttt{pre-commit}
configuration whose \texttt{no-commit-to-branch} hook protects \texttt{main} and
keeps development on feature branches, and a CPU \texttt{Dockerfile} reproduces
the environment so the experiments run identically inside a container. The
project carries an MIT licence and a \texttt{README} documenting every command.
